Survival Rate

When looking at the Fortune 500 from 1960, only 60 out of 500 corporations are still on the list2. Many of those companies operated on Business-to-Business (B2B) level and seemed immortal at the time. This illustrated that even big corporations struggle with continuous innovation and adaption to trends and needs. Even more so, as can be seen in the graph below 3, the expected lifetime of a company is gradually decreasing, and projected to be only 18 year in 20274.

%

overestimation of demand

Because of the so called "Bullwhip Effect", a demand of ↑5% on consumer level may be interpreted by the supply chains as a change in demand of up to ↑40%. Distorted information from one end of a supply chain to the other can lead to tremendous inefficiencies: excessive inventory investment, poor customer service, lost revenues, misguided capacity plans, ineffective transportation, and missed production schedules5. Above this, researched indicated that only +/- 9% of companies are fully prepared for supply chain disruptions such as these9.


Click to Visualise

Problem


Lack of Innovation

The reason behind the low survival rate of corporations in B2B settings is that corporations tend to start experiencing difficulties with innovation8 once they grow too large. This is mainly because these corporations shift from an innovation or technology driven mindset, to a risk-reduction mindset7. The companies do not dare to innovate because of the fear of making bad investment decision which could jeopardize their competitive advantage.

Furthermore, the reason behind the aforementioned Bullwhip effect is that corporations, as they grow too large, often lose sight of end-consumers because other companies within the B2B supply chain act as 'barriers'. This could be dangerous because the end-customer should be seen as key figure, seeing their needs, values and opinions will affect the entire supply chain9. Furthermore, because of the scale of the corporations, and the many stakeholders involved in a supply chain, companies often have to deal with conflict of interest.

core process

Our core value is to help industries which are not creative in essence, but are in need for innovation. Hereby our task is to revitalize your innovation strategy through design.

By using the Design Intervention as core process, we will overcome the challenge of bringing design thinking such as, human-centredness, experience & emotions to the core of your innovation strategy6.

For something new, stop doing something old

With complex problems, creativity requires courage, and change requires perseverance. we can help to set in motion the chain-reaction of innovation by making intricate problems tangible with the help of our ability to create a rock-solid vision. This is because, in order to be successful, innovation and creativity should be seen as a state of mind, and not the product of logical thought.

Step 1

01

Preparing the Ground


planning the intervention
Framing the Problem
A strategic design intervention will have an impact on the offer of the company, how the company works, and even for its vision and culture. This all is taken into account and a planning for the necessary structural and cultural changes are made.
Mapping the Company
When defining the problem, it is essential to understand the culture of the company and identify it's most prominent challenges in order to plan for the fitting innovation solution.
Mobilising Stakeholders
Especially within supply chain management, it is essential to identify the important stakeholders, involve them in the process, and prepare them to work with a design approach to innovation.

Co-Creation is key to success

In dynamic environments , that are subject to non-linear change, co-creation is key to success in innovation. We can help to create support for solutions within large corporations, and our resilience ensures an outcome that suits both the company's resources, and it's customer needs.

Step 2

02

Co-Creating Outcome


Executing Intervention
Managing the Process
It is important to choose the right tools and methods for the task. Each problem might require different approaches, but in a B2B setting the end-consumer should always be the underlying theme.
Adapting the Tools
After choosing the right tools and methods, it is important to customise the tools so they fit the problem. Furthermore, the methods should be adapted in order to co-create solutions with the relevant stakeholders.
Involving the Stakeholders
In order to co-create the outcome, the chosen tools and methods should be used together with the main stakeholders. It is important to involve them in order to create acceptance and commitment for the design approach.

Wire solution into brand DNA

Design and innovations is not solely about providing solutions. It is about changing a mindset, and implementing the solution seamlessly into the daily operations. We are able to create support for the solution, and gradually implement the solution into the Brand DNA, without disturbing the daily practices.

Step 3

03

Embedding Outcome


Executing Intervention
Evaluating Outcome
In order to create a lasting impact, it is of importance to evaluate whether the innovations works. If needed the solution can be improved in an iterative way. Hereby, the 'Design Intervention' process should not necessarily be seen as a linear, but rather a parallel process.
Educating Stakeholder
It is important to start embedding the design by changing the mindset. This is to make sure that, through a close collaboration with the stakeholders, the changes in the processes are made permanent. Because of the many different stakeholders, it is important to speak in their language, which is mainly a business language. The model will be explained through KPI's, and implemented into business models.
Create Acceptance
Lastly, it is essential to make sure that the strategic work that is executed is really adopted by the company in the long term. Not only the management, but the whole company should be convinced about the success of the outcomes, and the need to follow up on these outcomes. Especially to use design, and a human-centered approach, as the main driver of innovation.

Supply Chain Disruption G4S

01

Problem

in 2012, G4S, formerly Group 4 Securicor, experienced the effect of globalisation. Bad publicity shifted public opinion towards G4S, pressuring all of the stakeholders of its supply chain. The big amount, complexity and mutual dependence of corporations lead to reputational damage for all involved, a fragile supply chain, and a brittle relationship between end-consumers.

02

Solution

We recognised an increased need for proper risk management from a consumer perspective in order to retain a positive image and prevent supply chain disruptions. Hereby we implemented human-centeredness into the new Brand DNA, taking into account end-consumer relationships.

03

Result

The new Brand DNA not only prevented major business disruptions for G4S, but also ensured that the global security market is increasingly being recognised as an essential partner for outsourcing capital-intensive risk strategies.

Rewiring Strategy Maersk Line

01

Problem

Maersk Line, a (container shipping) lining company. sells distribution services to their customers through "agencies". These agencies formed barriers, which made Maersk Line prone to supply chain disruptions.

02

Solution

We identified that Maersk Line could seriously improve the efficiency of it's value chain by eliminating the middle man (agencies) for collaborations with big corporations. This way, Maersk would be in direct contact with it's customers, enabling them to responds better to the market's demand.

03

Result

The new strategy brought Maersk line closer to their customers, and enabled them to innovate directly with their clients. Furthermore, because of ruling out the fee of the middleman for the 'big fish', Maersk line's profits increased considerably.
